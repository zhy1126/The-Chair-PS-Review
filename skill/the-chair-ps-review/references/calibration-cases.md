# Anonymised Calibration Cases

These cases are synthetic summaries derived from multiple private samples. Do not reconstruct or disclose the source documents.

## Case A: The merit inventory

### Pattern

A roughly 1000-word law PS includes a high GPA, scholarships, a moot award, a research paper, several internships, an exchange, a regulatory programme, detailed career stages, and multiple programme modules. Nearly every experience is retrospectively described as evidence of fit with corporate and financial law.

### Expected diagnosis

- Structural positioning error: the essay tries to prove that the applicant is impressive rather than explain a precise match.
- Structural CV-likeness: too many experiences receive too little analytical depth.
- Weak main line: removing or reordering several experiences would not change the argument.
- Superficial match: modules are attached to experiences, but the applicant's unresolved question and training need remain unclear.
- Professionalism issue: GPA and scholarships duplicate other application materials and consume scarce words.

### Expected guidance

Ask the applicant to define one corporate-law problem or tension, select two or three experiences that genuinely developed that question, remove transcript-style evidence, and rebuild programme match through a specific capability gap.

Do not rewrite the essay.

## Case B: The focused policy trajectory

### Pattern

A social-policy applicant begins with a concrete observation about rural teacher retention, investigates incentive design using academic modelling and empirical evidence, tests a related mechanism in an organisational setting, recognises limits, and identifies programme resources needed to translate findings into policy.

### Expected diagnosis

- Strong fit thesis and coherent progression.
- Experiences perform different functions rather than repeat excellence claims.
- Quantitative evidence supports, rather than replaces, reflection.
- Check whether programme details are specific enough and whether the final claims overstate policy impact.

## Case C: The elegant but over-engineered narrative

### Pattern

A law applicant connects legal-aid work, corporate governance, moot arbitration, a law-firm matter, comparative study, and international arbitration through the idea of information asymmetry and procedural justice. The prose is strong, but some transitions imply that earlier experiences directly caused later interests despite uncertain chronology or role fit.

### Expected diagnosis

- Preserve the intellectual theme.
- Verify chronology, factual role, and whether each procedural claim occurred in the stated setting.
- Distinguish a genuine retrospective interpretation from an invented turning point.
- Remove experiences that require strained causal explanations.
- Require programme match to return to a specific research question, not general institutional prestige.

## Case D: Same background, different programmes

### Pattern

One applicant uses overlapping experiences in two essays: one for social policy and one for HRM. The material is valid for both, but each essay must organise it around a different core problem, analytical need, and future use.

### Expected diagnosis

Do not penalise reused experience by itself. Test whether the applicant has genuinely changed the argument:

- Does the central question change?
- Does the reflection select different implications?
- Does the programme resource answer a different need?
- Does the future use follow from that programme rather than a generic career plan?

Simple school-name or module substitution fails the test.
